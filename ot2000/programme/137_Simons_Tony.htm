Dovetailed Development

Workshop 170 minutes

Weaving together models and design techniques

Tony Simons

This workshop aims to pool the best experiences and identify the best practices in object-oriented development, using the UML notations. We shall be on the look-out for: alternative techniques that support the construction of particular models, the effect of developing models in different orders, the degree to which models are independent or support each other, helpful restrictions or alterations to the semantics of UML models, techniques which are mutually compatible or incompatible, and counter-examples which have been known to confuse developers! The goal is to identify and agree upon sets of compatible techniques and models that might constitute a realistic OO development process.

 Tony Simons was born in Leicester and educated at the Wyggeston Grammar School and Emmanuel College, Cambridge. In 1985 he was appointed as a Research Assistant, and from 1988 as Lecturer in Computer Science at the University of Sheffield, Department of Computer Science, where he is now Head of Teaching. He has taught Smalltalk, Eiffel and C++, analysis and design methods and artificial intelligence courses.

His early research work in the Speech and Hearing Research Group focused on object-oriented models of speech events and low-level phonetic decoding, with a certain amount of AI. Increasingly drawn to object-oriented languages themselves, he completed a PhD in object-oriented type theory and language design. Currently a member of the Verification and Testing Research Group, his research interests include object-oriented languages, analysis and design methods, type theory, verification and testing.

Tony has presented tutorials and workshops previously at ECOOP '93, '97 and '98; at OOPSLA '93 and '94 and OT '98 in the areas of object-oriented theory and analysis and design methods. He has presented technical papers at numerous conferences including OOPSLA, ECOOP and TOOLS and was a member of the OOPSLA '95 programme committee. He is a member of the OPEN Consortium, subject editor for Object-Oriented Systems and a contributor to the pUML group. Tony is the chief architect behind the Discovery Method for developing object-oriented systems and co-author of the encyclopaedia of object-oriented development techniques: The OPEN Toolbox of Techniques (Addison Wesley, 1998).

Topics

  • Goals of different stages of object-oriented development
  • Definition and description of techniques and models
  • Capturing and recording requirements and system events
  • Identifying and distributing system behaviour over objects
  • Transforming designs to create decoupled subsystems
  • Conversion of design documents into code and test suites
  • Model bias: supporting and conflicting perspectives
  • Sequencing: making one technique build upon another

Benefits

Participants will learn to step back from UML modelling and consider why different activities are carried out at different stages of the OO lifecycle. By appreciating these goals, they will be better able to identify and select a sequence of self-supporting techniques and models to build their own development process.
Session: Workshop 170 minutes Level: advanced
Audience:

Participants should be familiar with the UML notations and have some personal experience of what works and what does not work for them in object-oriented development, in order to contribute. Familiarity with event-driven, data-driven and responsibility-driven design approaches will help inform the debate and discussion.

 Max 36

Material

The documentation will summarise the premises of the workshop, outlining the goals of OOAD (assuming a medium-sized IS project). The materials will include thumbnail descriptions of the UML models and the different stages where they might be appropriate; thumbnail descriptions of different techniques for requirements capture, analysis, design, coding and testing, leaving space for variant models and new techniques.

Delivery

The workshop will produce a flowchart showing the preferred development path(s) chosen by the participants, indicating techniques (processes) and deliverables (data) and how these depend on each other. The workshop leader will write up any particularly striking insights regarding the compatibility of different techniques and models and distribute this via the web after the conference.

Format

The style will be brief introductory presentations from the front, followed by extended round-table discussions in up to 6 teams of 6 people each, concluding with sessions reporting back to the whole workshop, opening up the discussion.

Structure

The participants will be divided into a number of groups (up to 6
teams of 6 members) tasked with identifying best practice and
pitfalls encountered during OO development.  They will be supplied
with thumbnail descriptions of techniques and models, to which they
may add their own.

The first 90 minutes (up to the break) will be devoted to techniques
and models for requirements capture, object identification, data
and event modelling.  This breaks down into:  15 mins for the leader
to present the main concepts of:  goals, models, techniques;  50 mins
for groups to focus on a particular sub-area;  25 mins for the groups
to report back and present findings.

The second 90 minutes (after the break) will be devoted to techniques
and models for system-level design, coding and testing.  This breaks
down into:  15 mins for the leader to present the main concepts of:
goals, models, techniques;  40 mins for groups to focus on a particular
sub-area and 20 minutes for the groups to report back and present
findings.  The final 15 minutes will be spent attempting to synthesize
a process from the different identified development paths.

The groups will be tasked to different topics, depending on the size
of the attendance, and motivated to solve particular problems, of
which the following are exemplars:

Are interviews better than workshops for controlling requirements
capture?  Are activity diagrams better than use cases for modelling
business logic?  How does traditional ERM-style data modelling fit
with behaviour-centred object modelling?  How can state models and
data models be related?  Is OOD an elaborational or transformational
activity?  What kinds of model are needed to support distribution
of behaviours and reorganisation of subsystems?  On what basis are
subsystems identified and separated?  How far can we expect to
automate coding from design documents?  Where does planning for
testing fit into the process?
