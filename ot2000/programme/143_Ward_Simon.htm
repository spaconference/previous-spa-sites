Nomads, Shamans and Soothsayers.

Workshop 170 minutes

An exploration of Java architectures.

Simon Ward

The selection of Java for use on a project immediately presents the development team with more architectural decisions than maybe any other technology. Applets, servlets or application? RMI or CORBA? Beans? EJBs? It's a growing list. How do you balance the often conflicting demands of the users, their managers and the corporate IT Strategists when making the architectural decisions? Will the forthcoming J2EE Application Programming Manual from Sun help to ease the situation by providing a solution for every problem? The workshop will explore the various architectural options and consider the trade-offs that are made when making a decision. It will also demonstrate that just when you think you've got it right there's always someone willing to come along and put a spanner in the works.

A 'real life' project (due for delivery in March 2000) will provide the basis of the workshop. Participants will be formed into teams and vie with each other to produce the most flexible and resilient Java architecture in the face of ever more demanding requirements. At the end of each iteration of the project each teams proposals will be reviewed and discussed by the group.

While covering all aspects of Java architecture the workshop will place an emphasis on multi-tiered architectures and making enterprise systems available to users who have no fixed office accommodation.

Shaman: a priest or priestess who uses magic for the purpose of... controlling events.

Soothsayer: a person who predicts the future by magical, intuitive, or more rational means.

Simon Ward (simon.2.ward@bt.com)

BT

Simon Ward is a Lead Architect working for BT's Information Systems Engineering Division in Cardiff. He's spent more years developing software systems than he'd care to admit to in public. He's been working with OO systems since 1996, initially with C++ and latterly with Java. He's interested in distributed component-based systems and the problems of providing universal access to enterprise systems for all users regardless of their location or their means of access. He's also interested in the role that an Architect plays within a development team and how they need to balance the conflicting demands on the architecture from various sections of the corporate community.

Benefits

Participants will gain an insight into the pros and cons of various Java architectures. They will be able to explore the various options whilst at the same time having the opportunity to have their proposed solutions subjected to peer review during the group discussions.

Session: Workshop 170 minutes Level: intermediate
Audience: Architects, Designers and Developers who are using, or are interested in using, Java. Some familiarity with CORBA may also be useful but not essential. Max 24

Material

Slides giving a brief overview of the Java architectures to be considered. References to background material available on the WWW.

Delivery

The 'perfect' all-purpose Java architecture.

Format

How will you work? Protagonist, Adviser, Umpire, Chairperson, Scribe.
