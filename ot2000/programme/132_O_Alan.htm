Software Building

Workshop 170 minutes

The Road to a Software Architecture Worthy of the Name

Alan O'Callaghan

Kevlin Henney

The notion of non-trivial systems having software architectures is one that is gaining ground. But what is architecture? How can it be defined? More importantly how do you go about creating a software architecture that can be maintained successfully over the lifetime of a potentially long-lived system, and what are the organisational, social and process implications of generating such an architecture? What roles are played by layers, frameworks, patterns and components?

This workshop explores the relationship between building and software architecture, drawing in particular on functional architectures such as modern office environments for suitable comparison. The notions of space and boundaries in buildings have their counterparts in software, and offer an alternative way of considering traditional software concepts.

Alan O'Callaghan (aoc@dmu.ac.uk)

De Montfort University
 Alan O'Callaghan has been a Senior Lecturer at De Montfort University in the UK for ten years, having previously worked for the London Transport Executive, British Oxygen and Kodak. He has been working with object technology since 1991, and is well-known in the UK for his work on the migration of legacy systems to object technology, having pioneered developments in industrial projects in telecoms, the defence industry and the machine-tools industry. He has edited two books on object technology transfer, and is a regular columnist in the European SIGS journal, Application Development Advisor. He has presented talks, tutorials and workshops at Object Expo in London, OOP in Munich, Object World UK, Object Technology 9X and the OOPSLA Educators' Symposium as well as a number of industrial seminars and conferences.

Kevlin Henney (khenney@qatraining.com)

QA Training
 Kevlin Henney is a Principal Technologist with QA Training, where he consults, writes and delivers training courses and seminars in C++, Java, OOD, patterns, distributed objects, and component architectures. He has been working with object technology and some of its cultural offshoots for longer than he cares to remember, and tries to share/inflict these experiences with/on others at any opportunity, typically through the medium of articles, conference papers, conference talks, workshops and courses.

Topics

  • Points in common between building architecture and software architecture.
  • Concepts specific to building architecture, in particular office design.
  • Concepts familiar in software architecture, with some comparison against building architecture.

Benefits

The workshop is intended to arm the participant with both philosophical and practical notions of software architecture that will enable them to engage in the generation of appropriate software architectures for the problems they have to solve as a computer professional. It is also hoped to give them the opportunity to have a play with connecting toys in a conference environment.

Session: Workshop 170 minutes Level: intermediate
Audience: Software developers modicum of object-oriented or component-based design experience who are interested in alternative perspectives on the concepts they deal with in a technical environment. Max 30

Material

  • Presentation slides that are used within the workshop.
  • Additional notes, plus references, describing some of the ideas and positions taken by the presenters and others.

    Delivery

    Posters, and potential scope for sparking off BOFs.

    Format

    The session will start with presented material and progress to a more conventional workshop format, based on groups of 3 to 6 people working on a common problem. There will be some movement between groups and a wrap up session at the end.

    The intent is to make the session highly interactive, which may involve putting some worn metaphors to an empirical test, eg components as Lego bricks, Meccano, etc.
