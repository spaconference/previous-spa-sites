Crystal Clear Requirements with UML

Workshop 170 minutes

Make sure your model is complete and consistent

Alan Cameron Wills

Requirements capture is about getting as accurate a picture of possible of what the users want, before investing overmuch effort in design. Despite RAD techniques, there can still be a substantial delay between requirement and first delivery; and despite joint application development, it's still possible for substantial questions about exactly what the software should do to turn up unpleasantly late in the development process.

This workshop will explore techniques for exposing gaps and inconsistencies in requirements models, using high-precision UML to throw up important questions about requirements, that might otherwise have been glossed over or understood differently by all concerned.

Many texts on UML say little about how the different diagrams should cohere with each other. In this workshop, we will exercise a requirements analysis cycle in which cross-checks between the different parts of a model tell you when your requirements are consistent and inclusive.

This process, developed as part of the Catalysis method, has proved very effective in a variety of fields.

Alan Cameron Wills (alan@trireme.com)

TriReme International Ltd

Alan Cameron Wills has been a consultant in software development since 1991, working with a clients in telecoms, finance, and commerce in various parts of Europe and the USA. He is joint author of "Objects, Components and Frameworks in UML -- the Catalysis approach".

Alan likes taking pictures of people and sailing.

Topics

  • Review UML class and use-case notation
  • Snapshots: envisioning what happens
  • Use-cases and transactions
  • Postconditions, informal and formal
  • Actions to objects
  • OCL: being precise without being detailed
  • Statecharts
  • Objects to actions
  • Coherence between models
  • The analysis cycle
  • Practicalities

Benefits

  • Reduce late fudges
  • Reduce backtracking over requirements
  • Increase confidence in project direction
  • Make analysts more responsible for visible behaviour, instead of designers
