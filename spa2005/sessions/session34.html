SPA2005 session: Writing better scenarios by understanding narrative

One-line description:If we claim to use stories in design, we'd better understand how they work
 
Session format: Workshop [read about the different session types]
 
Abstract:The use of natural language narrative for capturing needs and designs is encouraged both in scenario-based development and in XP (stories).

The great thing about narrative is that readers bring so much knowledge to the process of filling in the gaps in understanding it, without even realising it, leading to design artefacts that are not crammed with verbiage and can be engaged with. The dangerous thing about narrative is that readers may fill in the gaps, without even realising it, and might accept promises you did not even know you had made. Both fiction readers and stakeholders regularly believe six impossible things before breakfast.

This workshop will look for lessons for writing good scenarios from both the literature about narrative and the related experiences of the participants.
 
Audience background:Anybody interested in agreeing and managing requirements and their solutions, which means: requirements engineers (if you're in engineering), business analysts (if you are in commercial IS), designers (if you are in usability or new media). And those accountable for their success.
 
Benefits of participating:Practices for effective scenario text, in the form of (part-cooked) patterns and anti-patterns: what must be said, what need not be said, and how to test what will taken for granted.

And perhaps ...

* Ideas for effectively linking scenarios by character, event or design object, to make dependencies clear for stakeholders, thus breaking out of use case silos.

* Ideas for educating stakeholders to be effective scenario readers. (After all, we learn how to read different kinds of fiction.)
 
Materials provided:A template for draft patterns.
