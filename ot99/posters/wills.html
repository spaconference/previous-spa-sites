Modelling components and architectures

Alan Cameron Wills

Trireme

alan@trireme.com

Component-based design (CBD) may be seen as two activities: building products by assembling components; and designing components. There are different emphases of robustness and rigour in the two modes. In product assembly, we rely on robustness in the carefully-crafted components; in component-building, there is more emphasis on investment in future reusability.

When building products from disparate components, it is important to start from clear models of what is required and the components available. The models can be used to clarify how the components are coupled together, and how a collection of components will meet the requirements.

In general, the models will all start from different views of the business concepts: for example, an accounts subsystem, a sales support component, and a dispatching support will all have their own views of what a customer is. These differing views need to be reconciled in order to plug the components together. This process reveals gaps and inconsistencies for which 'glue' has to be written.

A kit of components designed to work together needs a consistent architecture: that is, a set of rules and protocols about how components in the kit are intercoupled, and the concepts they and representations they share in common. This architectural definition is one of the most reused set of ideas in such a kit, and must be worked out and documented carefully. Again, appropriate modelling techniques are very valuable.

This workshop examines some of the ways in which modelling techniques can be applied to improve the effectiveness of CBD.
