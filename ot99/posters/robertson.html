A framework for modelling business processes

Stuart Robertson

Cumulus Systems

Object-oriented architectures and modelling methods are good at developing structural models for business applications. However it is in the area of modelling the dynamic behaviour of systems where complexity enters the notation of typical object-oriented analysis methods. For example in modelling methods such as Syntropy or UML, the type diagrams are relatively straight forward and can be used to expose a domain model to end users. However this cannot be said of the state diagrams, and related concepts such as events and guards, which are typically used by such methods to model dynamic behaviour. Problems occur when trying to combine such methods within an architectural framework for the definition and execution of business processing. This session describes recent experiences in this area.

The case study concerns the design of a large-scale financial application for the capital markets. The architecture developed has introduced a framework for the modelling of business processes in a distributed object-oriented environment. The framework developed not only provides a model for the definition of business processes, but also drives the actual execution of the corresponding processes.

The framework developed is designed to be used both as a development tool by Cumulus and as a configuration tool by the end-user in order to configure the system to meet the business requirements of the client organisation. The architecture also enables the corresponding business processes to be exposed to the end-user. The model must therefore necessarily be deliberately oriented at its target audience. Specifically it must not require a great deal of 'programming' or 'systems analytical' expertise. This has been the primary design consideration when developing this framework.

Key topics

The framework

- Model for the definition of business processes

- Relationship between business process definitions and the types defined by the business domain model

- Emphasis on the definition of operations and business processes, comparable to activity states and activity diagrams in UML notation

- Enabling the effect of a business process to be undone

Benefits of a generic framework for business processing

- Logging system behaviour

- Journalling the effect of business operations

- Recovery of business processes following system failure

- Modifying the functionality of the system

- Specifying business rules to ensure consistency of the model

Using the model

- Contention between end-user needs and defining system functionality

- Extending the model to introduce states and events: examples

- Dealing with part-aggregate relationships and sub-processes

- Dealing with relationships between business processes

- To what extent should such a framework utilise the concepts of state machines?

Stuart Robertson is a Technical Analyst with Cumulus Systems, a software house that develops systems for the financial sector. Cumulus is currently designing an integrated front and back office product in a totally object-oriented environment based around ORB, ODBMS, and developed in Smalltalk.
