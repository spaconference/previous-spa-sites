Concept Café

Sunday 1:00   Working group 300 minutes

Dialogue group exploring everything we ever thought, hoped or feared about objects but were afraid to say.

Martine Devos

James 0. Coplien

Working with users trying to surface real requirements (in terms of output not input), thinking about problems we often come in

situations where we feel that we do not get to the essence of the problem, that we get information fitting an "espoused theory" and not the real theory in use. In our own practice we use words and expect others to understand them, to give them the same meaning. Yet, we sometimes use these words within a different paradigm (some mechanical, some more dynamic). Even terms like pattern, or system thinking are used within different paradigms. One of the problems with paradigms -- in our practice as in a business domains -- is that words used in one paradigm may have a different meaning in another -- without us being conscious of it. We explore and experience techniques and methods for real dialogue using

metaphors and draw mind maps.

Martine Devos

Martine Devos recently joined EDS as Business Planning Consultant. She was director of the IS-department at Argo since 1992 and before has worked as teacher, as technical consultant and project leader to the Belgian Minister of Education and the Civil Service. As IS manager she introduced objects and she initiated, and coordinated the development of a framework and several applications using it. Her main focus is on the use of IS - and "softer software" - to support change programs and learning. Special interests are framework development, the use of patterns in organization, the human side of IS and facilitating workshops. She participated in, and organized, several workshops and panels on System Envisioning, the use of creativity in requirements developing. She is co-chair of EuroPlop'99.

James 0. Coplien

Jim Coplien is a Distinguished Member of Technical Staff at Bell Laboratories and a pattern laureate in the pattern community. Jim is the author of "Advanced C++ Programming Styles and Idioms", "Multi-Paradigm Design for C++" and he co-edited two

volumes of "Pattern Languages of Programming Design". Among his many contributions is his highly acclaimed seminal work on Organizational Patterns. He served as a program chair of ACM OOPSLA '96 and is a highly

demanded conference presenter and speaker. He is a certified as a facilitator by the Creative Problem Solving Group of Buffalo, New York.

Topics

  • use of metaphors and analogy
  • to talk about concepts, ideas, works that influenced our practice
  • with objects, patterns, ...
  • reflection, dialogue

  • suggestions for sub topics: patterns, requirements engineering, building a case for objects for non-technicians and management

Benefits

 Goal of this working group is learn about and adapt creative techniques and methods to facilitate dialogue and exploration of new possibilities. We create space to surface and challenge assumptions and learn from difference. We learn to use metaphors for sensitive reflection on concepts and methods. We share points of view, we share assumptions. We work together to adapt a method for requirements growing and system envisioning to reflect on objects, patterns and ideas that influence our

practice. We improve our techniques to represent ideas nonlinear using mind-mapping and meta-plan.

Level: basic to advancedAudience: people wanting to explore the use of lateral thinking in OO-practice

Material

 Slides presenting the idea of concept café, and "rules" for lateral thinking. Some pictures of sessions, and mind-maps produced with users. A table with a set of citations, photos, objects... for usage by late-comers

Delivery

 A group-mind-map on objects A PMI (evaluation focused on improvement) in the form of posters on things to keep of this techniques, improvements of this technique. We could present these in a 15 minutes session later on.

Format

Participants sit in a circle. They are expected to bring a text or picture and to use that to present some idea, doubt, problem or question about objects or something they consider an absolute keeper or a misconception they want to correct. Every participant -- NO fence sitters -- gets one (or more) rounds to present a short citation, a poem, a photo a drawing... to talk about a concept related to objects. Presenters read their excerpts or shows the object or picture and explains why this was chosen to talk about objects, his or her practice.

The use of metaphor helps to express (and detect) hidden ideas, presumptions... that are not always surfaced using definitions. The metaphors help place concepts in a larger context and may surface different paradigms. Presenters are not interrupted. The other participants take note -- using words and short phrases -- on CRC-cards. This way a map of concepts is build, with emphasis on strengths, weaknesses, opportunities and treats. Every presentation is followed by a short dialogue working with the metaphor... stimulating free-flowing inquiry. Several rounds putting emphasis or questioning concepts, problems... follow. We use post-its with question-marks, exclamation-marks...

Unprepared latecomers can participate -- the facilitators will bring a set of objects and pictures to choose from.

Structure

The concept café is a form of gentle dialogue (based on ideas by Peter Isaac -- the philosophers supper -- and Danah Zohar -- the concept café.

Each person takes a quote from a famous voice, a picture... interprets it, explains why he chose it, and shares some personal reflections on it.

Main goal is presenting and clarifying ideas using metaphors, mapping ideas, presenting the maps and reflecting on it. We do not work towards consensus.

Secondary goal is to experiment with this technique and improve it as a tool for discussing a domain (has been tried in business domains).
