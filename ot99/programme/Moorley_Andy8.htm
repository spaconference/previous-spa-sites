Architecture, Patterns and System

Sunday 1:00   Working Group 300 minutes

How might architectural patterns help us to create better architecture

Andy Moorley

Paul Dyson

More and more is being written about how patterns and architecture are related. People like Kent Beck and Ralph Johnson were suggesting as far back as 1994 that "Patterns Generate Architectures". However, they were talking about micro-level architecture being created by the sequential application of design patterns. More recently people have been suggesting that when we look at large distributed systems these sorts of patterns are at the wrong scale. We have therefore begun to see the publication of Architectural Patterns (e.g. "Blackboard" in Pattern-Oriented Software Architecture and "Isolation Layer" presented by Paul Dyson at the Patterns 98 conferences).

Creating an explicit architecture for a system has many stated advantages, but an area of key importance is how the architecture will allow the system to meets it's required quality attributes (e.g. performance, end-user functionality, required modifiability, required reliability etc).

This session will look at two different (perhaps contradictory, perhaps complementary) techniques for developing the architecture of a system. The first is a technique builds upon what we are calling 'Architectural Unit Operations' (small,well-defined, design operations such as "abstraction", "resource sharing"). These are then applied in a semi-mechanical process controlled by the desired quality attributes to produce an architectural style represented as a series of patterns. The second technique (taken from Extreme Programming) uses a rather looser specification of the system's required qualities and looks to 'grow' the architecture in an organic fashion.

Topics

  • Design Patterns and Architecture
  • System Quality Attributes
  • Unit operations
  • User stories
  • Identification of real-world architectural styles/patterns
  • Understanding the relationships between system quality attributes, unit operations and architectural patterns

Benefits

Participants will

  • Work towards a shared understanding of the relationships between patterns and architecture

  • Develop ideas of how required system qualities can be identified and expressed

  • Develop the ideas of (& hopefully a better name for!) Architectural Unit Operations.

  • Identify the comparitive benefits and liabities of the two techniques, and how and where they could be used

  • Work in small groups to progress abstract ideas towards a concrete and useful framework that they can apply in the real world.

    Level: advancedAudience: Software practitioners with architectural responsibilities. Those with a theoretical or teaching interest in patterns.

    • Material

    		 Attendees pack (distributed 3-4 weeks before conference) - introductory reading, questionairre (who are you, what do you do, what are your interests, etc.), invitation to submit a case study. All optional - people can still attend if they have read and submitted nothing but useful for those that want to prepare. Slides for various presentations Exercise sheets "Crib sheets" for the design exercise for those who want a little more guidance.

    Delivery

    		 We will write up a paper for publication on the Web etc which summarises the opinions and experiences of the group.

    Format

    Brief presentations on new/unfamiliar ideas interspersed with longer familiarisation and learning exercises. Group and individual reflection to draw together what we have learned.

    Structure

      (15) Introductions, presentation on patterns and architectures.

      (25) Breakout - Brain storm on architectural patterns seen by participants. In some case people will already be involved in patterns movement and be able to comment on some existing patterns which they feel are "architectural". In other cases we would ask groups to consider an architecture they know well and to see what sort of common ideas/design come out which could form the basis of architectural patterns.

      (5) Collect ideas into central list

      (10) Presentation on System Quality Attributes and Unit Operations

      (20) Worked example: Deriving "simple" patterns from unit operations. Based on Microsoft's Document/View architecture.

      [Break - 15 mins]


      (5) Presentation on User Stories and organic system growth

      (25) Worked example: Organic development of a simple architecture from a set of user stories

      Now some people investigate one approach while others investigate the other, in groups

      (45) Breakout

      For those looking at Unit Operations: Derive MVC architecture from unit operations. Apply further unit operations to create an improved architecture for GUI interaction.

      For those looking at User Stories: Staged development (on paper) of simple system specified by user stories. Iterative development of architecture and identification of patterns.

      [Break - 15 mins]


      (20) Report Back/Reflection Groups will compare, contrast and possibily combine the two approaches. At the end of this report back we will select one of the following next steps:

    • Work in two groups to investigate further each approach
    • Select one or both approaches and work on it or them (either in groups or all together)

      (45) Groups work by considering architectural case studies (either provided by the organisers or submitted in advance by participants) to see if we can map between the required/observed qualities and the target architectures using the techniques investigated in the previous parts of the session. Where architectures differ from targets, how do they compare (better, worse, just different).

      (20) Report back, wrap up and decisions on any future work.
