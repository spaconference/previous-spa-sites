Architecture, a metaphor for software structure

Tuesday 2:30   Working group 180 minutes

James 0. Coplien

Martine Devos

We talk about many kinds of software structure. Different aspects of structure have been emphasized over different periods of software evolution. The intial focus was on data structure and control flow. Subsequent paradigms focused on data flow, particulary in serial business processing applications. Module structure unified data and the operations most closely associated with it, leading to concerns of coupling and cohesion. Objects have created many synonyms for these structures based on allocation of responsibility, type models, and others. In the mean time, other dimensions of structure address the allocation of time: tasks, processes, autonomous actors, and other aspects of parallelism. Recent theories such as aspect-oriented programming, multi-paradigm design, and generative programming are all attempts to unify many of these structural aspects.

Structure is about architecture, and architecture is about structure. Architecture has also undergone evolution over the millenia, with the most radical changes having taken place in the last century. Most of these changes have been accelerated by availability of new materials and advances in building and engineering techniques. Each new style brings new questions of utility, aesthetics, and durability.

Recently, Alexander has tied his entire Weltanschauung to the concept of structure, offering evidence that it is the basis of beauty; this complements his long-held view that beauty and utility are inseparable (i.e., Ornament has Function). The pattern community is exploring some isomorphic relevance of this theory to software: how do patterns and centers map into the evolving structures of software systems?

Topics

    Interesting questions will be:

  • Are there strong isomorphisms in the evolution of these structures, or are the similarities incidental?

  • How did the role of architect evolve in each of these?

  • Is there a single metaphorical -- or even mythological - framework between these two disciplines?

  • What are the similarities and differences in the development processes for software and housing? Software and office building construction?

  • How do the reuse philosophies compare between software architecture and the architecture of the built world? How is each managed?

  • How far can the metaphor stretch?

  • What general things can be said about taking care in exercising the metaphor?

    We will choose from among these questions based on participant interest.

Benefits

 This working group will strive to identify parallels in historic developments in two schools of architecture - building architecture and software. Does the parallel hold? Where does is break? Participants will develop opinions about the applicability of the pattern discipline at different levels of interpretation. ranging from the codification of good practice to the relationship of geometry to beauty. We will also explore avenues for potential new software development styles based on those used in architecture, such as Construction On Site (as opposed to Prefabricated Housing).

Level: intermediate to advanced (but "wild geese" are welcome)Audience: software engineers, software architects,building architects, developers, project managers

Material

Photos, excerpts of texts describing the building architecture aspects

Delivery

 Posters of different aspects of the metaphor Digital Photos of the working groups, posters and material will be produced and made available to participants

Examples of the archicture metaphor in software practice

Format

 Introduction of the session and the building architecture metaphore -- slides, citations, examples, pictures and drawings.

Subgroups work on the analogy with software architecture, focusing on a specific area delineated by one or two of the above questions.

We will alternate presentation of aspects of the architectural metaphor and the working groups over the whole workshop.

Creative techniques (analogy, force-to-fit, brainwriting) will be used.

Working groups produce posters of the software-architecture analogy.

In a plenum session we make a plan for future action.

Structure
    alternating during the session (supported with different facilitation techniques):

  • Presentation building metaphor
  • Stretching the metaphor in small working groups
  • Use of visualization and experiments with photos, story-telling

    Recommended reading includes "How buildings learn" (Stewart Brand) and "The Timeless Way of Building" (Christopher Alexander)
