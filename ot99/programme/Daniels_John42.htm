Evolutionary Delivery

Tuesday afternoon (total 3 hours)

Tuesday 2:30Tutorial
TuesdayThink-Tank

John Daniels

It seems clear and obvious to me that adopting a software development process based on evolutionary delivery is by far the most effective way of controlling risk and meeting objectives in software projects. However, many organisations have not made the change, and many that have are only half-hearted in their adoption. Evolutionary delivery is a software process based on the following simple ideas:
  • The development is divided into a number of cycles, each delivering an ever-increasing subset of the total desired system functionality.
  • Early cycles deliver functionality that is spread thinly across all areas of the system and all of the hardware and tools, thus demonstrating end-to-end capability.
  • The content of each cycle is agreed beforehand and is fixed.
  • Users are involved in both agreeing cycle content and reviewing outcomes.
  • Cycle duration is firmly fixed. Although simple, these ideas have a knock-on effect. They imply better planning by project managers, higher commitment by users, and a willingness to compromise by developers.

    The tutorial session will explain the principles behind evolutionary delivery and set out a practical process by which it can be followed. Unfortunately, most software organisations seem unable or unwilling to apply the discipline required to make evolutionary delivery successful. The same objections to it come up time and again, such as "you can't deliver our system in cycles because it's only any use if you've built all of it", and "it's inefficient for the whole team to have to start and finish a cycle at the same time". I think it would be very useful to examine commonly heard objections and develop sound counter-arguments.

    The think-tank session will do exactly that.
