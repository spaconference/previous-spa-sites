Lateral Thinking and Patterns

Tuesday 9:00   Workshop 170 minutes

A Workshop on Lateral Thinking and Patterns

John Oldland

Lateral thinking was created by Edward de Bono over thirty years ago. Its basic premise is that normal 'vertical' thinking is too quick to dismiss ideas before their possibilities have been explored. Ideas which may seem impractical or even absurd may eventually lead to better practical solutions. While it has some similarity with 'brainstorming', it is much more disciplined.

Firstly, there are strict rules over what may be said at any time, somewhat like writer's workshops. For example, group sessions can be divided chronologically into differ periods of comment: positive comment, negative comment, connecting ideas and points of interest. This is only one of several ways of organising sessions.

Secondly, there are a number of unusual ways of generating ideas, such as unusual decomposition of the problem, reversing dependencies and processes in the problem, and adding random stimuli.

So what has this got to do with patterns ? Firstly, they are both semi-formal ways of general problem solving; and secondly they have both been criticized for being hyped and used in non-suitable problem areas. On the other hand there are a lot of differences. Whereas, lateral thinking is about creating new original ideas , pattern writing is about discovering existing ideas and conceptualising them.

The session will compare and contrast these two disciplines. Lateral thinking techniques will be explained and the audience will test their applicability to different issues in software projects. We will then explore the underlying notions of pattern writing and pattern languages.

John Oldland

 John Oldland comes from a pure mathematics background. He has been writing and designing transaction processing systems for the finance industry for the last twelve years. For the last five years he has been responsible for the business architecture of a distributed object-oriented system written in Smalltalk.

Topics

  • Description of Lateral Thinking
  • Comparison of lateral thinking with pattern writing
  • Patterns for idea generation in lateral thinking
  • Use of lateral thinking in software projects
  • Patterns in the abstract
  • Comparison with other disciplines
  • Conclusions about patterns

Benefits

Attendees should find some of the lateral thinking techniques useful, both in solo design and group design sessions. They should also gain a clearer understanding of what patterns arereally about and reassess their use of them.

Level: intermediateAudience: Some knowledge of the principles of patterns andpattern languages will be useful. Enthusiasm and an open mind are themost important qualities that attendees can bring to the session.

Material

 Discussion paper incorparating introduction, description of lateral thinking, lateral thinking patterns, appropriateness to software development, contrast with pattern writing, examples of comparison of pattern writing with other disciplines.

Delivery

Three sets of posters will be produced: Problem solutions using lateral thinking Appropriateness of lateral thinking to software projects What is pattern writing really about and why is it useful ?

Format

 Session will alternate between speaker presentation, group working and group presentation to the session.

I realise this proposal does not fit the mould of the traditional OT session, and would therefore, appreciate shepherding.

Structure

An initial introduction will explain the principals of lateral thinking. This will be followed by a short discussion contrasting this approach with the use of patterns. ( 20 mins.)

1. Part 1 ( 1 hour 15 mins.
)

There will then follow a description of some standard lateral thinking techniques for generating ideas in the form of a part of a pattern language.

The attendees will be divided into a number of groups who will use one or more of these techniques to tackle a one of a number of problems. These will include software design, component interfacing, documenting an architecture and creating user documentation.

Groups will present their solutions and discuss whether they found the technique useful or not.

2. Part 2 ( 1 hour 15 mins.
)

Part II will look at writing patterns, using patterns and pattern languages. I will start by asking "what is a pattern" ? We look at comparative techniques in other disciplines. For example is parallel evolution a good example of a pattern in biology ? Are algorithms very formal patterns ? How do patterns compare with analytical techniques used in the fields of music and art ?

Attendees will be asked to come up with there own ideas and analogies and then to answer some fundamental questions such as: Are patterns invented or discovered ? Do patterns appear because there are generic problems or is it only the solution which is generic ? Is it better to describe the problem a pattern solves in terms of forces or motivations ? What kind of structure should a pattern language have ?
