SPA Conference session: Diagnosability - Is it worth it?

One-line description:Sometimes it seems impossible to work out what is going wrong in a running system, is putting effort into diagnosability the answer?
 
Session format: Think tank (75 mins) [read about the different session types]
 
Abstract:The aim of this session is to explore costs and impact adding diagnosability to software systems to handle configuration, user error, system error or software bugs.

As software has become both more complicated and distributed the ability for a simple error message to help diagnose an problem is limited. The technical aspects of reporting errors are well known and even with all these in place it is not always enough. Sometimes it takes a concerted effort to reproduce a running environment and debug into the code to find what a problem is. Even during development it can be difficult and costly to find the cause of a problem, especially during integration or system testing.

Questions this session would seek to answer are:

- What are the defining features of a diagnosable system rather than one that just reports errors?

- What does it take to make software diagnosable? What is worth diagnosing, what is not?

- When should diagnosibility be considered, during development, when issues arise?

- What is the impact on the life-cycle costs, does it cost more to develop, does it make end-to-end testing a more efficient process, does it make deployment simpler, does it reduce the overall cost of the software?

- What is the impact on customers of the software, in particular does this help any of the customer roles more than others?

- At the end of the day is diagnosibility worth the extra effort rather than a simpler error message approach?

 
Audience background:Architects, Developers, Testers, in fact anyone who has worked with large complex systems who are (sometimes) unable to work out the cause of something going wrong in a system.
 
Benefits of participating:This session will provide participants an understanding of the impact of adding diagnosibility to software, the costs and the benefits, when it is worth considering and when it is not.
 
Materials provided:A small introduction and a set of questions to be considered by the group.
 
Process:The session will be started with a short presentation defining diagnosability and some generalities about why it may be beneficial or not. This will be followed by a consideration of the questions raised in the abstract.

The reminder of the session will then be divided into targeted discussions to answer the questions in turn.
 
Detailed timetable:The following is an indication of the structure that the discussions will take. It is expected that there will be flexibility in the schedule as more questions are raised and discussed. It is expected that the attendance will be relatively modest to allow the group to work as one, however a larger audience will be handled splitting the audience into groups.

00:00-00:05 A brief introduction

00:05-00:20 What is diagnosibility?

The aim of this segment is for the group to agree what diagnosibility means, what makes it different from error handling, how it is might be implemented.

00:20-00:35 What are the benefits?

The aim of this segment is to find the positive impacts based on the definition agreed in the first segment. Whom does it benefit? Developers, Testers, Customers, Operators? It is good for all problems? What costs does it reduce?

00:35-00:50 What are the costs?

The aim of this segment is to find the negative impacts. What does it make harder? Who is least helped? What costs does it increase?

00:50-00:75 Is it worth it?

This segment is provided to consider diagnosability in cost/benefit terms with the groundwork already performed in the previous segments. The aim is to answer the question: "is it worth it?" and to provide the reasoning for the answer. This will form the basis of the output of the session.
 
Outputs:The output will seek to answer the main question: is it worth it? The output will include a summary of the conclusions reached during the session.

A poster will be produced directly after the session and posted at the conference, further details of the session and the discussions will be added to the conference wiki either during or shortly after the conference.
 
History:This is a new session.
