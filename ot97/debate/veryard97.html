Design Pitfalls as Negative Patterns

Many patterns can be expressed in negative form: avoid XYZ. Experienced engineers have a personal set of negative patterns (or design pitfalls) that they search for when evaluating or testing other people’s designs. Some of these negative patterns have a corresponding positive pattern: "avoid XYZ, do PQR instead". If such patterns could be recognized by automated design tools, appropriate warnings and wizards could be provided.

John Daniels writes: 



> all patterns contained a negative element



This is true.  But I haven't seen much focus on this negative element before.



Two cases particularly interest me.



1) When the negative element of the pattern is much stronger than the positive element.  

This is true of many of Christopher Alexander's patterns, where the structures he dislikes are often expressed much more clearly and forcefully than the structures he recommends.



2) When the designer is unaware of having used a pattern at all.

The identification of negative patterns within an existing or proposed design becomes a powerful way of critiquing the design.



John Daniels also writes:



> Do you really believe this kind of automation is possible?



Yes.  Many design tools (such as CASE tools) issue warnings to the designer when they detect certain simple structures.

A design tool could, for example, detect loops in a complex software system that could result in runtime deadlocks.

My conjecture (to be examined at the OT 97 workshop) is that there are many unexplored opportunities for building such facilities into design tools.
