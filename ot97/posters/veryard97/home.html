Design Pitfalls as Negative Patterns

This page describes a workshop session that will be run as part of the Object Technology 97 conference in Oxford, April 14-16 1997.

Subject of Session

Many patterns can be expressed in negative form: avoid XYZ. Experienced engineers have a personal set of negative patterns (or design pitfalls) that they search for when evaluating or testing other people's designs. Some of these negative patterns have a corresponding positive pattern: "avoid XYZ, do PQR instead". If such patterns could be recognized by automated design tools, appropriate warnings and wizards could be provided.

The principle objective of the session is to evaluate the usefulness of the concept of negative pattern. We will discuss some commonly occuring negative patterns, and will discuss general ways (also patterns) in which negative patterns in a given design may be identified and treated. This discussion could continue afterwards by Internet, with a possible ultimate goal of developing a repository of negative patterns and guidelines for their use.

For general information about patterns and software patterns, visit the Patterns Home Page.

Deliverables

  1. Set of negative patterns, together with their positive correlates
  2. Set of patterns for the use of negative patterns

Target audience

  • Consultants experienced in inspecting and testing designs, wishing to share and formalize their experience.
  • Pattern creators.
  • Quality specialists.

Format and method of working

The workshop will attempt to adhere to the following timetable.

20 minutes: introduction by session leader
30 minutes: brainstorm negative patterns
30 minutes: brainstorm positive correlates to the negative patterns
40 minutes: brainstorm ways of using negative patterns
30 minutes: evaluation of the overall concept

Materials (Not yet implemented)

  • Initial examples to stimulate discussion.
  • Blank template forms for documenting patterns.

Session Leader

Richard Veryard has 20 years experience as a consultant, lecturer and author in the software industry, concentrating on tools and methods. His book on Information Modelling (Prentice Hall, 1992) contains a large number of analysis patterns, while his book on Information Coordination (Prentice Hall 1994) contains patterns of human organization and interfaces. More recently, he has developed a quality system methodology for Texas Instruments Software, and is currently working on methods to connect business process reengineering with component-based development.
