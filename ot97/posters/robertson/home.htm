Use Cases/Events for Discovering and Using Requirements Patterns





A Workshop for Object Technology 1997

Workshop Subject

The purpose of requirements patterns is to communicate requirements knowledge so that it can be reused. The most useful requirements patterns are those that are easily mappable to the early work of the requirements analyst. Use Cases/Events can be used early in a project to identify business related "chunks" of work. Use Cases/Events are also a convenient tool for packaging and accessing requirements patterns. This workshop explores how to use generic patterns to specify the requirements for a specific use case/event.

The potential of patterns was recognised by an architect, Christoper Alexander, who defined a pattern language for building a living environment. Object oriented designs have developed the idea of using patterns to define and communicate solutions to design problems. Lately analysts and requirements engineers have been exploring the potential of requirements patterns as a vehicle for reusing requirements. You can find more information on patterns at the pattern languages home page.


Target Audience

System Developers, Requirements Engineers, Systems Analysts, Systems Designers, Project Managers, Reuse Gurus - anyone who is involved in reuse or in requirements definition.

Objectives of the Workshop

The objective is for the participants to:

  • specify requirements for the case study, by using use case/event patterns
  • explore the advantages and disadvantages of using requirements patterns

Structure of the Workshop

The session will be run as a business game that simulates the definition of detailed requirements for renting movies.

• The Scenario

The participants are asked to play the parts of both users and requirements analysts by defining their requirements (both functional and non-functional) for a movie rental system. The choice of subject is driven by the need for a system with which everyone has had some experience.

• The Business Simulation

The business game simulates the idea of using requirements patterns to help start a project. The game explores ideas like: use cases/events as a vehicle for capturing requirements patterns, the composition of a requirements pattern, how detailed/abstract should the pattern be, advantages and disadvantages of using requirements patterns. The participants will work in groups. The session is divided into a number of work units. The session leader will introduce and review each work unit and provide guidance during the workshop:

  • Session leader defines use cases/events, requirements patterns, presents example patterns and objectives of workshop
  • Teams use a requirements pattern to define the functional requirements for a use case/event
  • Session leader introduces requirements template and discusses non-functional requirements
  • Teams define selected non-functional requirements using requirements template as a guide
  • Each team reviews requirements defined by another team
  • Exhibition for each team to present lessons learned, advantages and disadvantages




Materials


Participants will be provided with:

  • A summary of use cases/events and requirements patterns
  • Example requirements patterns
  • A summarised copy of the requirements template from the Volere requirements process

Suzanne Robertson

Suzanne Robertson is a principal of The Atlantic Systems Guild. She is a teacher and consultant specialising in modelling techniques for systems development. Her courses on requirements, systems analysis, design and problem solving are well known for their innovative workshops and business games. Suzanne is currently developing techniques for reusing requirements and assessing requirements specifications..

Suzanne has worked in information technology and has had varied experience as a programmer, analyst, designer and manager. Since 1978, she has consulted, done research and taught in Europe, Australia, the Far East and the United States. She specialises in helping organisations to adapt modern software development techniques to fit specific projects.

In 1983, the partnership of: Tom De Marco, Peter Hruschka, Tim Lister, Steve McMenamin, John Palmer, James Robertson, and Suzanne Robertson founded the Atlantic Systems Guild. The guild is a New York and London based think-tank, researching system development techniques and producing many well respected books and papers.

Suzanne is co-author of Complete Systems Analysis: the Workbook, the Textbook, the Answers (Dorset House, 1994), a two-volume text and case study that teaches the craft of systems analysis using examples from personal experience of working on a large project. Suzanne studied Information Processing at the New South Wales Institute of Technology, she is a member of the IEEE, the Australian Computer Society and is on the committee of the British Computer Society's Reuse Group.
