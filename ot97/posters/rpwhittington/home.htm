A Tutorial for Object Technology 97:

Business Objects are Much More than Technology

Led by Dick Whittington

Subject Matter

There is much talk these days about business (or enterprise) objects. Much interest derives from opportunities for greater system integration, through re-use of larger-grained components. But the potential benefits for business integration are no less significant. Object technology can be used to create a business architecture that delivers clear statements of purpose, responsibility and performance, well-defined relationships, and resilience to change. The creation and availability of such architectures significantly reduces risk in development projects. Requirements models can be tightly linked to organisational objectives, enabling exploration of alternative implementations.


This tutorial will address the use of business objects in organisation design, focusing upon the business benefits that can be achieved, modelling techniques that can be used, and cultural issues relating to the development process.

Target Audience

Those who are keen to understand how business risk can be managed through effective use of business object modelling, and how implementations can be tightly linked to organisational objectives. This will include: business analysts, project managers and business managers. A general awareness of OO principles is assumed.

Structure of the Tutorial

Introduction and Terminology

What is a business object? We will discuss the characteristics, and differentiate from software objects. Briefly:

  • Business objects represent business resources and capabilities. Examples include Customer, Product, Order, Opportunity, Diary, Delivery, Request, Complaint, StaffMember, …
  • A business object integrates behaviour and data that the business needs in order to fulfill its process responsibilities. So, for example, a Customer object integrates the behaviour and data that the business needs in order to acquire, engage and manage its customers.
  • This behaviour will be delivered in part by people, and in part through technology (i.e., communications and information systems). Business objects abstract away from implementation specifics. It is often important to be able to encapsulate requirement at a level that enables identification of implementation options, including responsibility distribution across people and technology.
  • They are normally large-grained composites, comprising several smaller objects. For example, a Customer object might contain Address and PersonName objects, together with references to Account and Diary objects.
  • Conceptually, business objects exhibit the same properties as software objects. Inheritance, encapsulation and polymorphism all apply in much the same way. The benefits of inheritance and polymorphism are very much in terms of consistency; and the benefits of encapsulation are very much in terms of clear process responsibility and management of change.
  • A business object architecture provides a framework for system object design. A business object will typically give rise to a number of system objects. Traceability of the system back to the business brings significant benefits during change management.

We will also briefly trace the history of object technology, from its roots in system implementation through increased abstraction and broader applicability.

What are the benefits of business objects? In particular, we will focus upon:

  • Business integration; the ability to achieve consistency of understanding and operation, within and between business processes. This is especially crucial in the context of change management.
  • Understanding and communication bridging the business / systems gap; the provision of notations and techniques that can engage management, operations roles and systems developers in a coherent debate. This applies during development and throughout continuing improvement.
  • Business risk management; integration and understanding both contribute to management of risk, a multi-dimensional concept that is ever-present during change. Looked at in this way, business objects can be viewed as a risk management strategy.

Participants will share in a short session of practical modelling, working in small teams. The precise nature of the practical work will take account of the number and backgrounds of people involved. The practical will wrap up with a short presentation and discussion.

Summary of Organisational Benefits

We will review the business benefits in the light of the practical examples.

Summary of Challenges

Introducing business object modelling into the organisation development process raises a number of cultural issues, including:

  • Many business people are not familiar with object concepts, nor even with the more general proposition of using (even semi-)formal notations to express business requirements. To be most effective, object approaches need to encourage active understanding and participation.
  • In many organisations, business people expect an involvement at the beginning and end of a development project. To be most effective, object approaches need to encourage wider and continual participation by the business.
  • IT people can be defensive or arrogant when their methods are exposed to the broader business. This can lead to resistance against wider involvement of business people. To be most effective, object approaches need to encourage developers to welcome a broader cultural mix within development teams.

We will review these and discuss ways to avoid potential resistance and win wider acceptance from business and systems communities.
