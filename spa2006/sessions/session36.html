SPA Conference session: Surviving Outsourcing: More than a zero-sum game?

One-line description:This workshop considers how, where and if outsourcing can be of positive benefit to participants and their organisations and how they might work with outsourced staff to ensure quality results.
 
 Session materials
 
Session format: Workshop [read about the different session types]
 
Abstract:Outsourcing, offshoring, nearshoring, smartsourcing and other variants of
getting work done by cheaper staff in other places are now common in most
large IT organisations. The question is, as reflective practitioners, can
we work with this development in order to deliver better, cheaper systems
for our customers or is such a decision always a negative development?

This workshop is divided into two halves in order to consider this question.

The first half of the workshop will ask participants to consider the various
variants of outsourcing as possible ways to develop IT systems and
ask them to consider the issues and positive or negative connotations of each. We
will look at the question from several perspectives, including an onshore
developer, development management, support staff and the ultimate customer.

In the second half of the workshop, we will assume that a decision has already
been made to outsource some significant part of the software development
effort for a particular project. In the light of this development,
participants will consider ways that they, as senior developers, architects,
managers or business analysts, can work with the outsourced staff in order
to ensure that an acceptable result is reached for the project's
stakeholders.
 
Audience background:Architects, designers, developers and managers involved in the development
of information systems. Ideally participants will have had some contact
with some form of outsourcing.
 
Benefits of participating:* The chance to consider the possible ways that different outsourced
development models could be applied and the possible implications
of each.

* The identification of a set of practices that could be used to control
and manage outsourced development in order to deliver an effective
system. Such practices could well be of use in more traditional
development situations too (such as using contract or software house
staff on a project).
 
Materials provided:* Presentation slides
* Worksheets for the exercises
 
Process:* Introduce the objectives of the session and the process to be used.
* Present a number of possible models of outsourced development, from
total organisational outsourcing to "body shopping" where commodity
resources are used from another organisation as part of a project.

* Split into up to 4 groups and undertake first group exercise: "Trick or Treat?".
In this exercise, the groups will consider some of the essential variables
for an outsourced project, including:

Size of project
Level of ceremony
Type of project
Domain (well-known vs obscure, technical vs business, local rules, etc.)

Given different types of outsourcing (such as remote developers,
entire technical team remote, entire project remote), the groups will
be asked to work out lists of issues that they could forsee
in different combinations of variables vs. outsourcing types
and which stakeholders (developers, managers, architects, business
sponsors etc.) are impacted by these issues in a positive or negative way.

* Collate the results of the first exercise using flip charts to
get a general view on types of project where outsourcing is considered
positive or negative.

* Split into two groups, undertake the second exercise: "That's easy for you to say!"
In this exercise, the groups are given a project scenario with a set of
variables as above. The "internal" team take on stakeholder roles while the
"external" team takes on development/project team roles (PM, designer,
team lead, etc.). The two groups will then prepare for and conduct an
initial "project kick-off meeting". Each group is given an "agenda" to pursue
in the course of this meeting. The objective is to see which of the issues
from the first exercise come up during the meeting and to see what proposed
solutions evolve.

* Present a couple of slides containing initial thoughts on possible
mechanisms for assessing, controlling and managing outsourced
development.

* In the original groups, undertake the third exercise: "Damage Limitation"
In this exercise, the groups will identify possible sets of practices
to address some of the issues discovered and to help them ensure cost effective,
low risk delivery of their project using the outsourced model.

* Each group presents their overall findings in rough categories:
(A) Can this outsourcing be a positive development?
(B) Who gets affected by it and how do their jobs change?
(C) How do the onshore staff run the project to get it delivered?

* Summary and recap.

on the web site afterwards
* Positive and negative uses of outsourcing models.
* Sets of practices to use when working with an outsourced project.
