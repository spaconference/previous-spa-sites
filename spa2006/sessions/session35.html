SPA Conference session: Functional Relational Programming: Waging war on complexity

One-line description:An examination of a potential new approach to building large systems
 
 Session materials
 
Session format: Workshop [read about the different session types]
 
Abstract:Complexity is the single biggest obstacle to the successful development of large-scale software systems. (Complexity here is meant in its traditional sense - difficulty of understanding rather than in the technical sense of computational complexity)

One of the major causes of complexity is the difficulty of managing state within a system. As a result, all major programming paradigms provide some means to facilitate handling state but functional programming - which in its pure form eschews state altogether - is arguably one of the more extreme. Possibly because of this, and the difficulty of applying some of its ideas to state-dependent systems, functional programming has seen only limited use in industry.

This is a shame because it has much to offer in terms of the war on complexity - large functional programs tend to be much easier to understand.

Functional Relational Programming is a new approach which attempts to bring the power of pure functional programming together with the benefits of clearly defined and managed state. It is hoped that the ideas will allow the development of large systems that are easier to understand (and hence to maintain) than more traditional stateful approaches.

The ideas should be applicable in any modern programming language.

This workshop will explore the issue of complexity in large systems and examine its relationship with the handling of state. We will introduce the core concepts of the Functional Relational Programming approach and give participants the opportunity to experiment with the ideas.
 
Audience background:Some experience in building large-scale systems and the problems involved would be useful, but not essential.
 
Benefits of participating:Participants will gain an overview of a possible new approach to struturing software systems. The primary benefit will be the chance to discuss this approach with attendees with the aim of identifying useful ideas that can be used in many different development settings.
