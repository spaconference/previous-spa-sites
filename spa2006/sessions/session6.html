SPA Conference session: Conservative XP In Anger

One-line description:This case study explores the relative merits, or demerits, of an augmented version of XP, known to its creators as Conservative XP.
 
 Session materials
 
Session format: Case study [read about the different session types]
 
Abstract:Conservative Extreme Programming (CXP) has the same values and principles as traditional Extreme Programming (XP) but utilises a different development cycle and a number of different practices. Because good requirements are a pre-requisite of CXP, as is the use of an embedded tester and test team, it can be utilised for projects that XP may not be suitable for.

This case study is about how a multi-million dollar, mission critical, integration project at one of Europe’s largest airlines was delivered using a plethora of tools and techniques, including CXP, with a team of ordinary programmers in a politically super-charged environment.

The objective is to disseminate knowledge of our approach and life on an interesting project. The project will not deliver until January 2006, therefore this could be a case study about a dramatic failure! However, the focus will be on practices that we used and lessons we learnt.
 
Audience background:Anybody interested in software who wants to discuss: methodology tailoring; quality assurance; coaching; and organisational change.

 
Benefits of participating:- Understanding how XP practices can be used in large enterprises.
- Learn how tools, such as FitNesse, can be integrated into a methodology to help deliver business value to geographically isolated customers/business analysts.
- How to coach a level 1 team to level 3 performance using intense on-boarding and in-project coaching.
- Understand techniques that can be use to affect change within an organisation.
- A chance to discuss all the gory details of software delivery.
 
Materials provided:Slides incorporating research notes and multi-media (photography and video) of the project.
 
Process:A number of topics will be presented, four in total. They will cover an introduction into the project under study and the CXP methodology; the business case for CXP, and when best to apply it; CXP practices; and finally, a wrap up that presents the benefits and drawbacks of this approach, and its overall success.

The presentations will be interspersed with group discussions to enable participants to give opinions, supportive or otherwise, and survey how participants have solved particular problems, such as coaching.

Timetable (75 minutes):
- Introduction (5 minutes). Here CXP and the project that is the focus of this study will be introduced.
- Group discussion/survey (10 minutes): The advantage and drawbacks of XP and agile methods.
- The Business Case for CXP(15 minutes). Here particular goals of an organisation are discussed, with group input, and how CXP can help realise them. The goals include: developer training, increased efficiency and, of course, high quality, working software.
- 5 minute break/informal questions.
- Group discussion/survey (10 minutes): Coaching. What are the best techniques for training team members?
- CXP by example (10 minutes). Here I will walk through the project, including coaching practices, and explain through examples the different practices of CXP and whether or not they were useful during the project.
- Group discussion/survey: the best techniques for organisational change.
- Wrap up (10 minutes): 'Methodologies don’t deliver projects, people do, but the methodology helps'. Here I will explain how much the methodology really contributed to the project success/failure. Additionally, did we change our parent organisations? Could we convince them to keep changing during the course of the project? What next?
